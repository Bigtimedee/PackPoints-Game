## The Thesis We're Building From

Eugenia Kuyda, via Acharya's essay: **"Most people aren't trying to save time. They're trying to spend it."**

AI is not primarily a labor-saving tool. It is a paintbrush — a technology of self-expression. The essay draws on Oliver Sacks: making something stretches a person across past, present, and future simultaneously. Consuming parks you in the now. The people who are happiest with AI are not the ones who automated their jobs. They are the ones who made something they couldn't have made before.

Execution just got cheap. The bottleneck on making is no longer skill, money, or permission. It is who has something to say. Software is about to be everywhere the way YouTube made video everywhere — built by people who never called themselves builders. A Kentucky electrician sells a $12.99 tool he built himself. A plumber cancels a $40K consulting contract because he can now build what he needed. Chris Dixon: **the next big thing starts out looking like a toy.** The essay ends: *"The toys were fine. But we always wanted to play with the boxes."*

## Where PackPTS Sits Today

PackPTS today ships the toy. You are served a masked card. You guess the player. You earn PackPTS. You redeem for real cards.

That is a good toy. The game mechanics work. The economy is real. The Daily 5 is sticky. But a toy is what you are given. It is not yet what you make.

The durable, human half of PackPTS — the part that compounds, that creates community, that makes an acquirer pay a billion dollars — is the game you get to build. We have not shipped that yet.

## The Making Layer

The Making Layer is the next major product surface. It gives any card collector the ability to build a playable game from their own collection in approximately three minutes, share it, and have other people play it. No technical skill required. The AI does the work; the human supplies the taste, the story, and the point of view.

### Snap-to-Set

The entry point. A collector photographs cards from their collection — phone camera, no special setup. AI identifies the cards, masks them to the correct difficulty level, and assembles a playable set. The maker gives it a name and writes one sentence about why these cards belong together. That sentence is the mixtape note. It ships.

This is the paintbrush. The maker does not need to know anything about how the game works under the hood. They need only to have a collection and something to say about it.

### Set-as-Mixtape

Every set carries its maker's identity. When you play a set someone else built, you are playing their taste. A 1987 Topps set built by a 55-year-old collector in Ohio who watched those players as a kid is a different object than a rookie-card set built by a 22-year-old who started collecting in 2020. Both are interesting. Neither is the default algorithmic feed.

The name, the note, the card choices — these are authorship signals. We surface them. The maker is credited every time someone plays their set.

### The 2am Moment

Someone finishes sorting a box of cards late at night. They photograph six of them, build a set, and share it. That share is not a retweet of someone else's content. It is a thing they made. It feels different to send and different to receive. We are designing for that feeling, not for raw impression volume.

### Rainy-Saturday Co-Creation

Two people — parent and child, two collectors, a dealer and a customer — build a set together. One nominates cards. The other approves or swaps. The resulting set belongs to both of them. We notify everyone who plays it.

### Commerce as Byproduct

When a maker's set is popular, people want to know where to get those cards. We surface that organically. A link to the card on Goldin or eBay appears in context — not as an ad, but as the answer to a natural question the player is already asking. Commerce follows great making. We do not lead with it.

## What We Are Not Building

The essay is a warning. The same AI that enables a paintbrush also enables slop — high-volume, low-taste content generated to capture attention rather than to say something. We are not building a tool that generates 500 sets automatically. We are not optimizing for the loudest output.

The attention economy produces engagement metrics that look healthy and communities that feel hollow. We are building for the opposite: lower volume, higher meaning, people who come back because they made something here and want to see who played it.

---

## North Star Metrics

**Sets Made** — the number of sets created by users (not by PackPTS staff) in a given period. This is the primary health signal for the Making Layer.

**Maker Rate** — the percentage of active users who have made at least one set. This is the conversion metric: are players becoming makers?

**Set Play Depth** — average number of plays per user-made set. This distinguishes sets people share and forget from sets that actually spread.

These replace time-in-app and MAU as primary signals. A product where 10% of users have made a set that was played by someone they don't know is a fundamentally different business than a product where 0% of users have made anything.

## The Acquisition Case

Fanatics, Goldin, PSA, eBay, and every major player in the card ecosystem has the same problem: their users are consumers. They buy, sell, grade, and watch. Nobody makes anything. There is no creative layer on top of the inventory layer.

PackPTS, with the Making Layer live, becomes the place where card culture makes things. That is a category that does not currently exist. An acquirer is not buying a game. They are buying the creative community that formed around the game — the makers, the sets, the culture that accumulated because people had somewhere to put their taste.

The toy gets you to the conversation. The boxes are the company.

## Build Order

1. **Now (shipped):** The toy — Daily 5, 1v1, Solo, Founders Pass, PackPTS economy, store, referrals.
2. **Next:** Snap-to-Set MVP — photograph → identify → mask → name → share. Single flow, no branching.
3. **Then:** Set-as-Mixtape identity layer — maker credit, play notifications, set pages.
4. **Then:** Co-creation and social signals — collaborative sets, play depth surfacing.
5. **Then:** Commerce integration — contextual card links from within sets.

We do not build steps 3–5 until step 2 has real makers using it. Maker Rate is the gate.
